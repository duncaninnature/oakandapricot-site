San José Street Tree Pilot Study

Overview

This study was motivated by curiosity about the role that San José residents play in urban forest stewardship. I was particularly interested in public right-of-way (ROW) trees in park strips—the area between sidewalk and street—which adjacent property owners are responsible for maintaining.

Sampling

  • The study was limited to San José Council District 10.
  • The target population was trees estimated to have been planted roughly 5–10 years prior to 2025.
  • I visited every tree in San José’s public GIS street tree inventory that had a trunk diameter of less than 2 inches in 2020 and was planted in a park strip.
  • At each site, I recorded whether the original tree was still present, whether a new tree had replaced it, or whether the planting site was vacant.
  • For new trees, I recorded the replacement species.

Hypothesis

Survival rates among recently planted park strip trees significantly vary across species.

Overall Results

Across the surveyed sites, 297 of 1,310 original trees were lost.

Future Research Questions

  • How do these results compare to other areas of San José and to the city as a whole?
  • What site, species, or management factors might explain why some trees perform substantially better or worse than others?
  • How might resident tree replacement decisions influence long-term canopy outcomes and San José’s Community Forest Management goals?
