San José Street Tree Pilot Study

A pilot look at young street tree survival, replacement choices, and persistent vacant planting sites in San José Council District 10.

Overview

This study was motivated by curiosity about the role that San José residents play in urban forest stewardship. I was particularly interested in public right-of-way trees in park strips—the area between sidewalk and street—which adjacent property owners are responsible for maintaining.

The analysis is organized into two groups: recently planted young trees from the 2020 inventory, and a separate group of dead, stump, and vacant planting-site records from the 2020 inventory. Together, these groups offer a preliminary look at both tree establishment and what happens after street tree loss or vacancy.

Group 1

This group includes public street trees in District 10 that were small in 2020 and likely represented relatively recent plantings. In 2025, each site was checked to determine whether the original tree was still present, whether a new tree had replaced it, or whether the site was vacant.

Group 2

Dead, Stump, and Vacant Street Tree Sites: 2020 to 2025

A second analysis focused on 2020 inventory records showing places where a street tree was dead, missing, or reduced to a stump rather than recently planted young trees. After removing a small number of unclear records, 353 sites were reviewed: 158 with a known former tree species, 185 already listed as vacant, and 10 listed as stumps.

127 New Trees in 2025
226 Vacant in 2025
64.0% Still Vacant
Important note: not every record in this group was a dead tree. Many were already listed as empty planting spaces in 2020. This section is best understood as a look at what happened to missing-tree sites over time: whether they were replanted or stayed empty.

Dead/Vacant Site Outcomes by 2020 Record Type

The clearest pattern is that records with a known former species were much more likely to have a replacement tree by 2025 than records already listed as vacant in 2020. In other words, once a site appears as simply vacant in the inventory, it may be less likely to be replanted within the following several years.

Former Species Outcomes Among Known Dead Trees

Among records where the former tree species was identifiable, replacement outcomes varied by taxon. Some patterns likely reflect site management context as much as species biology. For example, the Fraxinus angustifolia “Raywood” records were concentrated at one frontage where multiple replacement Pistacia chinensis were observed, so that pattern should not be generalized as a citywide replacement preference.

What the Two Groups Suggest Together

Young-tree establishment

Most small 2020 trees were still present in 2025, but outcomes varied by species and site context. The data are strongest as a pilot snapshot of establishment outcomes, not as proof that one species is universally superior to another.

Vacancy persistence

The dead/vacant analysis points to a different problem: once a planting site becomes vacant, it may remain vacant for years. This can create long-lasting canopy gaps even when individual young-tree survival is relatively high.

Together, the findings suggest that street tree canopy outcomes depend on both establishment success and the speed or likelihood of replacement after loss. Future work could compare these patterns across other San José districts, examine socioeconomic and management context, and evaluate whether replacement choices are narrowing or diversifying the street tree palette over time.

Future Research Questions

  • How do these patterns compare to other areas of San José and to the city as a whole?
  • What site, species, or management factors explain why some young trees persist while others are lost?
  • How often do vacant street tree sites remain unplanted over longer time periods?
  • Are some sites repeatedly replanted while other vacancies disappear from active replacement attention?
  • How do resident, HOA, and institutional replacement decisions influence long-term canopy outcomes?
