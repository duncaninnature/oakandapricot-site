San José Street Tree Pilot Field Study

A neighborhood-scale field study examining the status of young street trees in San José Council District 10 approximately five years after inventory.

Purpose

In summer 2025, I revisited a defined group of San José street tree sites to better understand early survival, replacement, and vacancy patterns. The project focused on small-diameter trees recorded in the City’s 2020 street tree inventory, using field verification to assess what remained, what had been replaced, and where planting sites were vacant.

Methods

  • Field survey conducted in Summer 2025 across San José Council District 10.
  • Targeted street tree sites recorded in the 2020 City inventory as <2" DBH to approximate recent plantings.
  • Focused on park strip trees; also reviewed sites labeled “N/A” and “Unassigned,” which field verification showed frequently corresponded to park strip locations.
  • Systematically revisited eligible sites to assess current status: same tree, replacement tree, or vacant site.
  • This effort functioned as a near-census of the defined subgroup within the study area.
  • DBH was used as a proxy for age, with recognition that growth rates vary by species, irrigation, and site constraints.

Key Findings

1,374 street tree sites surveyed
77% same tree remained
23% vacant or replaced
14% vacant sites
10% new replacement trees

These results describe one district and one defined subgroup of street tree sites. They should be interpreted as a pilot snapshot, not a citywide trend.

Species Patterns

  • Lagerstroemia indica (crape myrtle) appeared both resilient in place and commonly selected as a replacement tree.
  • Crape myrtle had a relatively low observed loss rate in this dataset and was also the most common identified replacement taxon.
  • Some taxa showed higher observed loss rates within this sample, including Prunus, Koelreuteria bipinnata, Ligustrum lucidum, and Jacaranda mimosifolia.
  • These patterns may reflect differences in species tolerance, planting site conditions, irrigation, maintenance, and neighborhood planting preferences.

Interpretation

The data suggest that many small-diameter street trees recorded in 2020 were still present in 2025, while a substantial minority of sites had either become vacant or been replanted. The vacancy rate is especially important because an empty planting site represents delayed or absent canopy recovery.

The apparent success of crape myrtle in this sample may reflect both its relative tolerance of constrained street environments and its continued use in replacement planting palettes. However, these observations should be interpreted cautiously because site conditions, irrigation, species growth rates, and maintenance histories vary widely.

Future Questions

  • How do early street tree survival and replacement rates vary across San José districts?
  • How strongly do planting space, irrigation access, and surrounding land use affect establishment?
  • Are certain species or size classes more reliable in constrained park strip environments?
  • How can QGIS and QField support efficient, repeatable urban forest monitoring at neighborhood scale?

About This Work

This pilot field study was conducted independently as part of Oak & Apricot’s broader interest in public-interest tree research for residents of the Santa Clara Valley. The goal is to make local urban forest patterns more visible, understandable, and useful for future stewardship.
